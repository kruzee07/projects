US Medical Insurance Costs

A Data Analysis Project


From: Codecademy

By: Krutarth Ghuge

Project Date: June 2024

Project Description

For this project, I investigated a medical insurance costs dataset in a .csv file (insurance.csv) using the Python skills that I have developed so far. This project was programmed using the Visual Studio Code editor and has been pushed to my Github repo.

Project Goals

  1. To assess the data collected in the csv file based on the factors mentioned.
  2. How a factor affects the medical charges on the patient.
  3. Does being healthy reduce medical charges?
  4. More children = more bills?
  5. Does your sex affect medical charges?

Data

Headers

  1. Age of person (numerical attribute): age
  2. Sex (categorical attr. - male / female): sex
  3. Body Mass Index (numerical attr.): bmi
  4. Number of children (numerical attr.): children
  5. Smoker (categorical attr. - yes / no): smoker
  6. Region of the US the person resides (categorical attr. - Northwest, Northeast, Southeast, Southwest): region
  7. Medical charges on insurance (numerical attr.): charges

Entries

There are a total of 1338 data entries (people).

Points to note

  1. No missing data.
  2. Seven columns.
  3. Some numerical, some categorical.

Analysis

I have performed some basic and intermediate analysis on the given data.
From the dataset, it is possible to conduct various analyses ranging from the use of just one attribute to all of them. For this project, I am performing (writing) functions or doing mathematical calculations to analyze and further visualize my findings.
Our data with the highest interest is the charges column, which is the ultimate base of this research file (most analysis includes this column).

Analysis that I performed

  1. Whether smokers have higher medical charges than non-smokers.
  2. How the medical charges change with age.
  3. How BMI across different regions of the US affect medical charges.
  4. The trend between the number of children and medical charges.

Evaluation

Analysis 1 - Whether smokers have higher medical charges than non-smokers

Data calculated

  • The total number of people in dataset = 1338
  • Total number of males and females = 676, 662 respectively
  • Total number of smokers and non-smokers = 274, 1064 respectively
  • Total number of male smokers and female smokers = 159, 115 respectively
  • The total medical charges = $17,755,824.99
  • The total medical charges for smokers and non-smokers = $8781763.52, $8974061.47 respectively
  • The average charges for smokers and non-smokers = $32050.23, $8434.27 respectively
For this finding, I went deep to find out all the small scale calculations part required for this analysis. The analysis turned out to be more mathematical than programmable.
Concluding with our findings, the results show that smokers had a higher average medical charge than the non-smokers. Although the total charges for each group were near about the same, due to a small number of smokers in the dataset, their average charge soared.

Analysis 2 - How the medical charges change with age

Data calculated

  • Age 54 had the highest total medical charges across US of $63770.43
  • Age 18 had the lowest total medical charges across US of $1121.87
  • Age 64 had the highest average medical charges across US of $23275.53 per person
  • Age 21 had the lowest average medical charges across US of $4730.46 per person
With that and the plotted graph, we can see that in entirety, the average medical charges per person increases with age.
Here I have created several functions to perform analysis on the dataset with the given project scope. In turn I also plotted a line graph to visualize the findings.

Analysis 3 - How BMI across different regions of the US affect medical charges

Data calculated

  • The BMI with the highest medical charge is 47.41
  • The BMI with the lowest medical charge is 23.21

    • The average charges for each BMI range are
  • Underweight = $8852.20
  • Healthy Weight = $10379.50
  • Overweight = $11017.60
  • Obesity = $15448.68

    • The average charges for each region are
  • NorthWest = $12417.58
  • NorthEast = $13406.38
  • SouthEast = $14735.41
  • SouthWest = $12346.94
The pie charts in the diagram (scroll below to Outputs -> Analysis 3) show the distribution of BMIs (percentages) across each different region of the dataset.
We can fairly conclude that a large portion of the US is obese and overweight and a very minute portion is underweight or healthy.
What is also peculiar is that the average medical charges increase as we go from low BMI (underweight) towards high BMI (obesity), disregarding the fact that there is a healthy BMI range in between.
The average medical charges across the four regions are also very similar, the highest however being for the South East region, and the lowest for the South West.

However, considering the real world, even if the people with 5 children on average are saving up on their medical charges, their living expenses and education costs for their children would on average be greater than those with fewer children.
